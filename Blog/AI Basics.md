# What is AI?

**Artificial Intelligence** (AI) refers to the emulation of human thought processes within computers. AI has been a subject of study, speculation, and mythologization since before computers were invented.[^3]

## Not just chatbots
The term "AI" in recent years has been used as an umbrella term for a range of technologies that are interrelated but distinct. 
- **Large Language Models** (LLMs) are programs that can interpret the meaning of an input and generate large amounts of syntactically correct[^1] text. 
- **Machine Learning** (ML) is a technique of "teaching" a computer to perform some task (identify objects in an image, map a travel route, or [play the board game Go](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AlphaGo_versus_Lee_Sedol)).
- **Neural Networks** are programs mean to mimic the structure of a brain, i.e. by processing an input through a series of "nodes" that influence the output, based on training data.

# What should I be aware of when using AI?
## Privacy
**Do not provide sensitive information to AI models**. User inputs to AI services are stored by the operators of such services, and are often up-cycled into future generations of AI models. This means any sensitive information— like names, personally identifying information, or financial data— can be stolen in a typical cyberattack or "distilled" from later models.

## Trust
The use of AI-generated content in public-, customer-, or funder-facing content can negatively impact the perceived trustworthiness of you and your organization. 

High-profile failures of AI, such as in cases where lawyers cite cases hallucinated by chatbots in their work, influence the overall perceptions of AI by the general public.

## Externalities
### Impact of AI development on public information repositories
AI developers require enormous amounts of information to "train" their models. AI companies fill this need by "scraping" the internet, or using automated systems to download large amounts of information very quickly by hopping from website to website through hyperlinks.

Since users expect up-to-the-minute information from AI chatbots, and each competing AI developer runs their own "scraping" operation, websites that host information for public benefit, like Wikipedia, national libraries, museums, schools, and galleries. The computers that host these public information repositories struggle to serve the unprecedented wave of information-hungry scrapers alongside human visitors, leading to server outages (Weinberger 2025).

### Rising Costs
The cost of AI subscriptions is expected to rise over time.[^citation needed]

AI services require: 1) Large amounts of data, 2) large amounts of specialized computer ships known as GPUs, and 3) large amounts of electricity. These three things cost money. The current wave of AI companies and services is fueled by investment in the tech sector with the expectation of returns. As expectations lower and competition declines, the investment currently floating the AI wave will decline. The services AI companies offer for free today will, as investment declines, be offered at a cost.
